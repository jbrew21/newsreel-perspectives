How Perspectives Works

Our methodology for tracking 232 voices across the political spectrum and grouping them by what they actually say, not who they are.

01

How It Works

Every day, we collect public posts from 232 voices across 7 platforms. Politicians, creators, journalists, podcasters, policy experts. We pull their posts, categorize each one by topic, then group voices into argument clusters for every story.

The result: for any story in the news, you can see who is talking about it and what they are actually saying, organized not by party or ideology but by the specific argument they are making.

02

Why No Left/Right Labels

We do not slap a permanent "left" or "right" label on anyone. That framing is reductive, and it is often wrong.

Instead, our AI analyzes what each person is actually saying on each story and groups them into 4-6 argument clusters. A voice might land in "Military Supporters" on one story and "Government Skeptics" on another. That is not inconsistency. That is how real people think.

Positions are fluid, not tribal. Our system reflects that.

Example

On a defense spending story, a senator might cluster with "Deterrence Advocates." On a surveillance bill the next day, that same senator clusters with "Civil Liberties Defenders." Both are accurate. A permanent label would miss one of them.

03

How Clusters Are Named

Every cluster name is generated using language its members would use to describe themselves, not language their opponents would use. This is enforced in the AI prompt and reviewable by editors.

The goal is descriptive accuracy, not editorial spin. If someone in the cluster would object to the name, the name is wrong.

04

Editorial Review

AI does the first pass. Humans have the final say.

Every story goes through an editorial review dashboard where human editors can:

  • Flag biased or loaded cluster names
  • Override cluster names with better alternatives
  • Flag unfair counter-narratives
  • Add editorial notes for context

These overrides feed back into the pipeline, improving future AI output. The system learns from editorial corrections over time.

05

Heat Score

Stories are ranked 0-100 using a composite heat score that measures how much meaningful disagreement a story is generating. It is not just volume. A story with 50 voices all saying the same thing scores lower than one with 20 voices split across opposing arguments.

Voice Count
How many voices are discussing this story, weighted on a log scale so early voices matter more.
Disagreement
Shannon entropy across clusters. Higher when voices are evenly spread across multiple positions.
Cross-Pollination
When ideological opposites land in the same cluster, something interesting is happening.
Quote Directness
Voices making direct, specific claims score higher than vague commentary.
AI Confidence
How confident the categorization model is in its topic matching and cluster assignments.
06

What We Track

232 voices across politicians, creators, journalists, podcasters, and policy experts. All public content. No private messages. Nothing behind paywalls.

Collection runs daily. Each voice is monitored across all platforms where they have a public presence. A single voice might have posts from X, a YouTube video, and a Substack article, all on the same story, all surfaced together.

Sources

X (via RSS), YouTube (RSS + transcripts), Bluesky (API), Instagram, TikTok, Substack, and podcast transcripts. Everything is public and linkable back to the original post.

07

What This Is Not

Perspectives is a tool for seeing the full conversation around a story. It is not a replacement for judgment.

  • Not a fact-checker. We show what people are saying. We do not verify whether what they are saying is true.
  • Not a bias rater. We do not score outlets or voices on a bias scale. We group arguments, not people.
  • Not Ground News. We do not rate news sources as left, center, or right. We track individual voices and their actual positions, story by story.

We show what people are actually saying, grouped by argument, so you can see the full conversation. What you do with that is up to you.
